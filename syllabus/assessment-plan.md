# Assessment Plan

This assessment plan frames **Quantitative Data Management and Workflow for Social Scientists** as a noncredit IBS CU Boulder microcredential or digital badge. It can also support a 1-credit graduate adaptation with additional grading policies.

## Assessment Philosophy

Assessment should focus on demonstrated workflow competency rather than memorization of syntax. Students should show that they can start from raw synthetic teaching inputs, record data decisions in code, protect source files, check their work, and produce analysis-ready handoff materials that another researcher can understand.

The recommended microcredential grading model is **complete/incomplete with revision allowed**. A portfolio is complete when it demonstrates the required competencies at a level appropriate for early graduate social science training.

## Pathway Options

Students may complete graded work through one of three pathways:

- Stata pathway: submit Stata scripts and Stata-generated evidence.
- R pathway: submit R scripts and R-generated evidence.
- Dual Stata/R pathway: submit both Stata and R workflows for extra practice or because an instructor requires both.

All students should understand both pathways conceptually. Unless a specific offering requires both, badge evidence is required in one primary pathway.

## Module Assignments

Each module includes a student-facing assignment. Module assignments are designed to build pieces of the final capstone portfolio.

Module assignments typically ask students to submit:

- a completed primary-pathway lab script,
- a short memo or reflection,
- evidence that a log, output file, report, or diagnostic was created,
- and a completed or referenced checklist.

Module assignments should be treated as formative assessment in the noncredit badge version. They give students practice and help instructors identify problems before the capstone.

## Module Checklists

Each module includes a checklist. Checklists are not intended as busywork. They help students confirm that they can:

- run scripts from the repository root,
- use relative paths,
- avoid manual edits to source data,
- create pathway-appropriate outputs,
- document decisions,
- and recognize when a workflow is ready for the next module.

For badge administration, instructors may require students to submit completed checklists, self-attest completion, or use checklists as preparation for capstone review.

Instructor review of smaller module assignments and checklists should follow `rubrics/module-checklist-rubric.md`. In the noncredit badge version, these reviews are primarily formative unless IBS or the instructor defines them as required badge evidence.

## Capstone Data Management Portfolio

The final assessment is the capstone data management portfolio in `modules/10-capstone/`. The capstone requires students to take raw synthetic inputs and produce a transparent, reproducible, analysis-ready dataset with documentation.

Students submit:

1. A reproducible Stata do-file or R script for the student's primary pathway, or both scripts for a required or optional dual pathway.
2. An analysis-ready dataset.
3. A data dictionary or codebook.
4. A QA checklist or QA report.
5. A brief workflow memo explaining major data decisions.
6. Evidence that they checked appends, merges, missing data, variable coding, and sample restrictions.

Generated data files, logs, and outputs should normally remain outside version control in ignored folders such as `data/working/`, `data/output/`, and `logs/`. If instructors need to review generated files, they should specify whether students upload them through an LMS, shared drive, or another approved mechanism.

## What Counts As Badge Evidence

Badge evidence should show that the student can perform the workflow, not just describe it. Acceptable evidence includes:

- scripts that run from the repository root,
- logs or text records showing script execution,
- analysis-ready output files generated by the script,
- codebooks or data dictionaries,
- QA reports with checks and warnings,
- sample construction tables,
- merge or append diagnostics,
- short memos explaining data decisions,
- and checklist responses tied to module or capstone competencies.

Evidence must use synthetic or publicly distributable teaching data only. Students should not submit private, restricted, licensed, confidential, or student data.

## Completion Threshold

The recommended threshold for earning the microcredential badge is:

- completion of orientation,
- completion of module assignments or equivalent instructor-approved evidence,
- submission of a capstone portfolio,
- and capstone review showing that all core badge competencies are met.

For internal review, IBS should decide whether module assignments are required for every student or whether the capstone portfolio alone can serve as summative evidence after students complete the learning sequence.

## Competency Evaluation

Instructors should evaluate whether the capstone portfolio demonstrates competency in the following areas:

- project organization and reproducible workflow design,
- raw data protection,
- importing and inspecting synthetic data,
- variable cleaning, typing, labeling, and recoding,
- missing-data handling and analytic sample construction,
- reshaping or restructuring data where applicable,
- appending and merging/joining files with diagnostic checks,
- QA checks and unresolved issue documentation,
- reproducible output generation,
- data dictionary or codebook quality,
- workflow memo clarity,
- and appropriate use of the student's selected software pathway.

Evaluation should prioritize evidence of transparent, defensible decisions. A portfolio does not need to be perfect, but it should make remaining issues visible and should not hide or silently drop cases.

The capstone portfolio should be evaluated with `rubrics/capstone-rubric.md`, which uses four competency-based levels: Complete / badge-ready, Minor revision needed, Major revision needed, and Not yet demonstrated.

## Revision And Resubmission Policy

The recommended microcredential policy is revision-friendly:

- Students whose capstone portfolios do not yet meet competency should receive targeted feedback.
- Students should be allowed to revise and resubmit within a defined window.
- Feedback should identify missing evidence, unclear documentation, failed checks, or reproducibility problems.
- Resubmissions should include a brief note explaining what changed.

For a 1-credit adaptation, instructors should define how revisions affect grades, deadlines, and incomplete work.

## Instructor Review Process

Recommended review steps:

1. Confirm that the student used the approved synthetic teaching data.
2. Confirm that the submitted script runs from the project root or that the student clearly documents any required setup.
3. Confirm that raw files were not manually edited.
4. Review the generated analysis-ready dataset or output evidence.
5. Review the data dictionary or codebook.
6. Review QA evidence for appends, merges, missing data, variable coding, and sample restrictions.
7. Review the workflow memo for plain-language explanation of major decisions.
8. Determine whether the portfolio meets badge competencies, needs revision, or requires instructor consultation.

## Adaptation For A 1-Credit Course

For a 1-credit graduate course, the same assessment structure can be converted into a graded framework. A possible grade distribution is:

- module assignments and checklists: 40 percent,
- participation, lab engagement, or peer review: 20 percent,
- capstone data management portfolio: 40 percent.

The exact grade weights, attendance policy, late policy, resubmission rules, and transcript requirements should be determined by IBS and the academic unit offering the course.

## Decisions For IBS Or Instructor Review

Before launch, instructors or IBS leadership should decide:

- the final badge competency statements,
- whether the badge requires all module assignments or only the capstone portfolio as summative evidence,
- whether a specific offering follows the standard Stata/R primary-pathway policy or requires dual submissions,
- where students submit generated outputs that should not be committed to GitHub,
- how many revision attempts are allowed,
- whether peer review is required,
- how long students have to complete a self-paced badge,
- and whether any local policy language is required for accessibility, academic integrity, or data security.
